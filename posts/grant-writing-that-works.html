Grant Writing That Works: What Funders Actually Want to Read

MN
Murewa Newo
Marketing Lead, Scribble Green
| April 2026 · 7 min read
💰

Most grant proposals don’t fail because the ideas are bad. They fail because the ideas are poorly communicated. Funders are not just evaluating what you want to do — they are evaluating whether you understand their priorities, constraints, and expectations.

After reviewing dozens of successful and unsuccessful applications across sectors, one pattern becomes clear: strong proposals are not necessarily the most complex. They are the most aligned.

Funders Are Not Looking for Ideas — They’re Looking for Fit

A common mistake in grant writing is treating the proposal like a pitch deck. Applicants try to impress with ambition, scale, or technical depth. But funders are typically asking a simpler question: does this project solve the problem we care about, in a way we believe will work?

Alignment matters more than originality. A modest but well-targeted project will outperform an ambitious but misaligned one almost every time.

"The strongest proposals don’t try to stand out. They try to fit — precisely, clearly, and convincingly."

Clarity Beats Complexity

Reviewers often read dozens of applications in a limited timeframe. If your proposal is difficult to follow, it will not be rewarded for its sophistication — it will be penalised for its friction.

  • Use simple, direct language wherever possible
  • Define technical terms clearly
  • Structure your proposal so that it can be skimmed
  • Make your objectives immediately understandable

If a reviewer has to reread a paragraph to understand your point, you’ve already lost ground.

Evidence Is Your Strongest Asset

Funders are inherently risk-averse. They are not just funding ideas — they are funding execution. This is why evidence matters.

Strong proposals demonstrate:

  • Past success or relevant experience
  • Clear understanding of the problem space
  • Feasible timelines and methodologies
  • Realistic budgets tied to outcomes

The goal is to reduce uncertainty. Every section of your proposal should answer the implicit question: “Why should we trust you to deliver this?”

The Narrative Matters More Than You Think

Even in technical or research-heavy proposals, storytelling plays a role. Not in a dramatic sense, but in how clearly you connect problem, solution, and impact.

A strong narrative ensures that your proposal feels coherent rather than fragmented. It helps reviewers understand not just what you are doing, but why it matters.

Common Mistakes That Undermine Strong Proposals

  • Being too vague about expected outcomes
  • Overloading the proposal with unnecessary detail
  • Ignoring the funder’s specific guidelines
  • Failing to justify budget allocations

These are not minor issues. They signal a lack of preparation — and funders notice.

What Winning Proposals Do Differently

The proposals that succeed tend to share a few characteristics: they are clear, focused, evidence-driven, and aligned with funder priorities. They respect the reviewer’s time and make decision-making easy.

Grant writing is not just about writing. It is about understanding incentives, reducing uncertainty, and communicating value in the clearest possible way.
