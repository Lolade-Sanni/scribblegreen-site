The ATS Problem: Why 75% of CVs Never Reach a Human Eye

SU
Steven Umurie
CTO, Scribble Green
| April 2026 · 6 min read
📄

Most job seekers assume their CV is being evaluated by a recruiter. In reality, it is usually evaluated by software first. Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS) act as the first filter — and most CVs do not pass.

This isn’t because candidates are unqualified. It’s because their CVs are not structured in a way that machines can interpret effectively.

What an ATS Actually Does

An ATS scans CVs for keywords, structure, and relevance. It ranks candidates based on how well their documents match the job description.

This means your CV is not being “read” in the traditional sense. It is being parsed, scored, and filtered before a human ever sees it.

"If your CV is not written for both humans and machines, it will likely reach neither."

Why Most CVs Fail

  • Poor formatting that ATS cannot parse
  • Missing or weak keyword alignment with job descriptions
  • Use of graphics, tables, or columns that break parsing
  • Generic content that lacks role-specific relevance

Even highly qualified candidates get filtered out at this stage simply because their CVs are not machine-readable.

Keywords Are Not Optional

ATS systems rely heavily on keywords. These are typically pulled directly from the job description — skills, tools, qualifications, and responsibilities.

But keyword stuffing doesn’t work. The keywords must be used naturally, within meaningful descriptions of your experience.

Structure Matters More Than Design

Many candidates prioritise visual appeal over functionality. While design matters for human readers, ATS systems prefer simplicity.

  • Use standard section headings (e.g., Experience, Education)
  • Avoid complex layouts
  • Stick to clean, linear formatting

A well-structured CV will outperform a visually impressive but unreadable one.

The Human Layer Still Exists

Passing the ATS is only the first step. Once your CV reaches a recruiter, it still needs to communicate value clearly and quickly.

This is where many candidates make a second mistake — they optimise for ATS but forget the human reader.

What a Strong CV Does

The most effective CVs operate on two levels:

  • They are optimised for ATS scanning
  • They are compelling for human reviewers

This balance is what separates candidates who get shortlisted from those who don’t.

Final Thought

The hiring process has changed, but most CVs haven’t. Understanding how ATS systems work is no longer optional — it is a basic requirement for navigating modern recruitment.
